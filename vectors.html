Chapter 01 — Vectors

You already know
what a vector is.

You've felt one. The moment you kick a ball, push a box, or feel wind on your face — that force has a direction and a strength. That's a vector. Mathematics just gave it a name.

Physical intuition

A force has direction

Push a box straight right — it slides right. Push it straight up — it lifts. But push it at an angle, and something interesting happens: it moves both right and up simultaneously.

The box doesn't care about your angle. It responds to two independent questions: how much horizontal force? and how much vertical force? Your diagonal push is secretly two separate pushes happening at the same time.

The key insight: Any vector — any force, velocity, or displacement — can be split into independent components. One for each dimension. The diagonal movement is just both happening simultaneously.

This splitting is called resolving a vector. And it's the most important thing you can do with one.

The abstraction

So what is a vector?

An arrow. That's it. Something with a direction and a magnitude (length). We write it as two numbers because we're in 2D space — one number per dimension.

Those two numbers aren't the arrow itself. They're the answer to: how much horizontal, how much vertical? They're the resolved components. The arrow comes first — the numbers are just how we describe it.

Formal definition
A vector is an element of a vector space — an object with magnitude and direction. In 2D, we write it as (x, y) where x and y are its components along the basis vectors î and ĵ.

Geometrically: an arrow from the origin to the point (x, y).
Physically: a quantity that has both size and direction — force, velocity, displacement, field strength.
The chess analogy

The numbers depend on the ruler

Here's what most courses skip: the numbers (x, y) aren't absolute. They're travel instructions — how many steps of î, how many steps of ĵ.

Change î and ĵ — use a different basis, a differently shaped grid — and the same physical arrow gets completely different numbers. The force didn't change. The ruler did.

Think of it like chess. A knight moves in an L. A bishop moves diagonally. Give both the same instruction — "go to square (3,2)" — and they end up in completely different places, because their movement rules are different. The coordinate is always relative to the movement rule.

Standard basis
î = (1,0), ĵ = (0,1)
Square grid. The ruler most people assume.
Different basis
î = (1,1), ĵ = (1,−1)
Parallelogram grid. Same plane, different ruler.
Same arrow
The physical force doesn't change. Only the numbers describing it change.
Same Earth
Moving the prime meridian doesn't move any country. It just changes every coordinate.
Summary

What to remember

A vector is an arrow — direction and magnitude. When you write it as (x, y), those numbers are its components: how much of each basis direction. The components depend on your basis. The arrow doesn't.

Resolving a vector = expressing it in terms of your basis vectors.
Same arrow. Different basis. Different numbers. The physics stays the same.

This is why physicists and engineers live in linear algebra. Every time they resolve a force, decompose a velocity, or break down a field — they're doing exactly this. Expressing one arrow in terms of their chosen movement rules.
